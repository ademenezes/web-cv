About Me

Economist with over twelve years of experience in causal impact evaluation, spatial econometrics, and programming-intensive research across water, climate, and gender.

Currently an Economist at the World Bank's Water Global Practice, I lead causal impact evaluations of private sector participation in water infrastructure across 2,920 utilities in Brazil using staggered difference-in-differences and event studies. I also lead the IBNET global tariff study covering 187 countries and nearly 10,000 utilities, the EqualAqua gender analysis linking 4.1 million worker records to 5,344 utilities, and the Global WaterSAP platform aggregating data from 6 international sources. I built a harmonized performance database consolidating 33,509 utilities from 32 national regulators.

My PhD research at LSE examines how climate change redistributes the burden of vector-borne disease using causal inference and spatial econometrics. I have built an automated systematic review engine screening 53,000+ papers, designed interactive dashboards in R Shiny and Python Dash, and taught Applied Environmental Economics to 300+ students for three years at LSE.

Privatization Impact on Water Utilities

Causal econometrics study evaluating the impact of private sector participation on water utility performance in Brazil. Implements 5 identification strategies including TWFE, event studies, IPW-DiD, Callaway-Sant'Anna group-time ATTs, and Goodman-Bacon decomposition. Gender Dynamics in Water Workforce

Python • R • Oaxaca-Blinder • FFL Quantile • RDD

Econometric gender study examining employment and wage gaps across Brazil's water and sanitation sector using 4.1M RAIS worker records (2006-2023). Applies Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition, unconditional quantile regression (FFL), regression discontinuity at the 100-employee threshold, and panel fixed effects across 5,344 utilities.
